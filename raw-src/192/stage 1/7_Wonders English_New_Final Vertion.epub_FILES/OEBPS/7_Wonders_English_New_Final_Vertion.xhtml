Mustard

Mustard

(Sasive/Sarason)

This little black hard seed bursts when heated. It is used in our day-to-day Indian cuisine and has been a popular food spice for more than 3,000 years. The name ‘mustard’ is aptly derived from the Latin word ‘mustum’ meaning ‘must’. Its mention in the “Tales from Buddha” signifies the importance of this spice. Lord Buddha advised a woman, grieving the loss of her only son, to bring mustard seeds from any household which has not experienced death. Her inability to find such a household helped her to realize that death is inevitable. I remember enacting this tale in a popular Kannada play “Saavillada Maneya Saasive” during my school days.

Mustard has an extremely pungent smell. My late mother-in-law used to grind the mustard seeds for her famous pickles and would give me a whiff of the ground paste on the next day. The heady smell would always make me feel a little dizzy. Hence, mustard is always used in very small quantities in all our dishes.

Mustard products are extremely popular for speeding up the human metabolism and aids in digestion. Massage with mustard oil increases arterial pressure and stimulates blood circulation and hence, it is used to treat blood clots and easemuscular pains. Ground mustard applied on any aching part of the body produces a lot of heat and helps to heal the injury.

During my visit to the Northern Indian states of Punjab and Haryana
I remember seeing the yellow flowered mustard plants grown in the fields. Mustard is grown in this area of our country and hence, the popular Punjabi dishes ‘Makki ki Roti’ and ‘Sarson ka Saag’ originated from this region.

Sasami/Sasive/Hasi/Pachadi

Sasami in Tulu or Sasive in Kannada is a very popular dish for Mangaloreans and Kannadigas. The name is derived by the vegetable used in it, like the sauthe (cucumber) sasami, beetroot sasami, and pineapple sasami etc. In Tamil and Malayalam it is known as pachadi.

  • 1 teaspoon Mustard
  • 3 Byadagi or Kasmiri red chilli
  • 1 cup (100 grams) Fresh grated
    coconut
  • ¼ teaspoon Tamarind paste
  • Salt to taste

    Mix all the ingredients and grind it into a smooth paste. This masala paste is added to a variety of vegetables or fruits to make different types of sasamis.

    Cucumber Sasami

    Mix chopped cucumber to the ground masala paste to make raw cucumber sasami. In the same way, we can make raw carrot sasami, raw onion sasami and red tomato sasami. We can also roast some uddu-happala or urad-dal-papad, break it into small pieces and then mix it with the masala base to make happala sasami.

    Ripe Mango Sasami

    Squeeze out the juice of the mango and add the masala paste. Mix well. There is no need to boil this mixture. Whole green, ripe grapes can also be used instead of mangoes.

    Red Harive Soppu Sasami (Amaranth Leaves)

    This famous sasami with red harive soppu is very popular in dakshina Karnataka. Clean the tender harive soppu (Amaranth leaves) chop into small pieces. Then cook it. Add little salt and ground masala paste and boil it for a minute.

    Beetroot Sasami

    Grate the beetroot and boil it in ½ a cup of water. Add the salt and ground masala paste and cook for a minute.

    Pineapple Sasami

    Chop pineapple into small pieces, cook it, add the masala paste and boil it for a couple of minutes.

    All these above sasamis are very tasty and can be eaten with plain rice as well as with chapatis. To enhance the taste you can temper the sasami with coconut oil and hing.

    • Sasami can be made with boiled raw vegetables.
    • If you use sour fruits like mangoes or grapes to make this dish, do not add tamarind.
    • If you use sweet vegetables e.g. beetroot or carrot, there is no need to add
      jaggery.
    • Mustard must be added judiciously otherwise you may end up having a bitter dish.
    • Byadgi or Kashmiri chillies are always less spicy and bright red in colour. Make sure you select the same quality of chillies in your dish.
    • Tempering with oil, mustard, hing and curry leaves always adds a special flavour to the dish. Although it is optional, a dish without tempering or voggarane is an incomplete dish!!
    • 150 grams of above mentioned vegetables or fruits makes sasami to serve 4 people.
    • Total time for the preparation of this dish is about 10 to 15 minutes (including cutting and boiling).
    • Raw sasami has to be prepared and served immediately.

    Pickles

    Pickles have a long shelf–life and spicy wonderful taste. There are hundreds of varieties of pickles in our country. I would like to highlight some popular pickles of South Canara.

    Adangai Uppinkai (Pickle)

  • 2 or 500 grams Raw mangos
  • 4 teaspoons Mustard
  • ¼ teaspoon Methi seeds
  • chana dal size Asafoetida (Hing)
  • 15-20 Byadgi chilli
  • 4 teaspoons Salt
  • a pinch of Turmeric powder

    Chop raw mangoes into small pieces. The mango can be chopped with or without the skin. Add 200 ml of water to salt and turmeric and boil for 5 minutes. Switch off the stove and after a minute, add the mango pieces. Cool it overnight.

    Roast all the other ingredients with hing and powder this mixture. Add the powder to the boiled and cooled pickle pieces and keep this for a day before using it.

    This is a very popular and tasty pickle of dakshina Karnataka. As this is an instant pickle, it does not last very long. It must be refrigerated for long term use.

    Ganji (soft rice) made with coconut milk is eaten with Adangai pickle in south Karnataka.

    The masala powder made for pickle can be used for many other dishes like Mango rice, Tomato rice etc.

    Adding temper with 2 teaspoons of oil is optional.

    Raw Mango Chutney (Mango Hindi)

  • 4 teaspoons Mustard seeds
  • 6 teaspoons Red chilli powder
  • 4 teaspoons Salt
  • 1 kg Scraped Mango
  • a pinch of Asafoetida (Hing)

    After cutting raw mango pickle pieces, there are lot of mango pulp left around mango kernel (goratu/ote). This pulp can be used to make mango hindi.

    I learnt this from my grandmother as she did not believe in wasting anything. Add mangopulp and salt layer by layer in a bottle and keep it for 2 days.

    Now grind mustard seeds, chilli powder & hing to powder form and add the bottled mango pulp to it and grind it all together and preserve it in refrigerator. This is very yummy with chapatis, puris, or dosas. Adding temper with 2 teaspoons of oil is optional.

    Mango Achaar

  • 2 kgs Mango
  • 100 grams Yellow mustard or
    yellow broken mustard seeds
  • 100 grams Red chilli powder
  • 1 teaspoon Fenugreek (methi) seeds
  • 100 grams Salt
  • 3 grams Asafoetida (Hing)
  • 100 grams Refined oil
  • 2 teaspoons whole mustard
  • pinch of Turmeric powder

    Cut mangoes to pickle size pieces. Roast methi seeds with hing and grind with the broken yellow mustard seeds (if you are using whole mustard seeds, grind the mixture coarsely) and then add the other spices. Mix all the ingredients with salt and chilli powder and then add mango pieces. Keep this in a big bottle. Stir it daily for 8 days. Heat the oil and 2 teaspoons of mustard. When these crackle, add hing powder and allow the oil to cool. Pour this cooled oil on the pickle in the bottle.

    Always use a dry spoon when you are taking the pickle from the jar otherwise the pickle will get spoilt with fungus growth due to moisture.

    This tasty pickle can be had with any dish.

    Mango Avakai (Andhra style)

  • 2 kgs Mangoes
  • 100 grams Mustard (powder)
  • 100 grams Red chilli powder
  • 2 teaspoons Methi
  • 100 grams Salt
  • 100 grams Refind oil
  • 3 teaspoons whole mustard
  • 3 grams Asafoetida (Hing)
  • a pinch of (1/4 teaspoon)
    Turmeric powder

    If you want to do avakai with the kernel (tough inside part of the mango) then it is better to get the mangoes cut by the seller only. They will cut it easily. Only you have to take clean cloth to clean the outer surface. Same way you have to clean the cut mangoes with cloth. Otherwise cut the mangoes to pickle size pieces at home.

    Mix mango pieces with salt, mustard powder, chilli powder, methi seeds, turmeric powder and hing powder. Keep it in a clean big bottle. Stir it daily for eight days.

    Heat the oil with 3 spoon of mustard till it crackle. Add little hing powder and keep to cool. Fill the pickle in a clean bottle and then top it up with the cooled oil.

    Soaked methi seeds will give different flavour. If you want add garlic then keep 30-50 numbers of cloves in sunlight for a day and add it to the pickle while mixing.

    Gonkura Pickle

    Gonkura is a leafy vegetable sour by taste. In Kannada it is known as huli soppu, pundi soppu and in Marathi its name is Ambati baji. This pickle can be preserved for many months.

  • 1 bunch or 500 grams
    Gonkura soppu
  • 3 teaspoons Mustard seeds
  • 2 teaspoons Salt
  • a pinch of Turmeric powder
  • 4 teaspoons Refined oil
  • 1 teaspoon Methi seeds
  • 4 teaspoons Red chilli powder

The most famous are pumpkin red or white, Mangalore cucumber, French Beans, Thondekai, Potato and onion mix, carrot and beans mix, or mix of the any hard vegetables. You can also use whole grams like green gram, channa, kidney beans etc to make this sambar. There are two varieties of sambars that can be prepared. One is known as roasted masala sambar and another is raw masala (Hasi masala in Kannada) sambar.

    Roasted Masala Sambar

  • ½ kg Vegetable of any
    kind as written above
  • 50 grams Tur dal
  • ½ teaspoon Tamarind paste
  • Salt to taste
  • ½ teaspoon Jaggery (optional)

    For the masala :

  • 2 teaspoons Coriander seeds
  • ½ teaspoon Cumin seeds
  • 20 Methi by count
  • 1 teaspoon Urad dal
  • 4-6 Byadagi Red Chilli
  • 20 Curry leaves
  • 1 cup (50 grams) Grated coconut

    Cut any one kind of vegetable into cubes of one inch size. For vegetables like Beans cut them into two inch length. Boil them without adding salt or tamarind. For vegetables like Ladies finger and Brinjal, add salt and tamarind while boiling. Otherwise, sambar becomes sticky and gooey. Add turmeric powder right in the beginning itself. When the vegetables become transparent, add salt, tamarind juice and jaggery.

    Always take care not to overcook/boil the vegetables. Especially for sambar preparation, the maintainance of the right texture is important and by practice this can be attained.

    Boil tur dal separately in a cooker and later this has to be added to the cooked vegetables.

    Now roast all the masala items along with a portion of curry leaves, in a low flame until this turns into light orange. Add grated coconut and grind well. Add this to the cooked vegetables and dal. Add the remaining curry leaves and boil this for another 4-5 minutes in a vessel. This has to be well boiled as it doesn’t get spoilt and can last for the whole day, especially during summer. Stir the contents regularly orelse the contents may settledown at the bottom of the vessel.

    If you are using refrigerated grated coconut, remember to add it with the masala in order to bring it to the room temperature.

    While preparing whole grain sambar (Gassi in Tulu), soak the grain overnight. Later, the next day, this can be cooked in the cooker. Follow the similar method for further preparation. This method also applies when you use green leafy vegetables.

    Preparation and cooking time :

    20-25 minutes Serve : 4

    Raw Masala Sambar

    For this Sambar only a few selective types of vegetables such as beans, raw banana or suran (Yam) can be used. Another very popular vegetable used for this is the raw Jack fruit sambar. If using raw Jackfruit, remember to select a tender one (not overgrown) and to remove the outer skin and cut the inner portion to 2 inch sized pieces. The method of preparing this sambar is the same as the above, only the masala would change.

    for the masala:

  • 1 teaspoon Coriander seeds
  • 1 ½ teaspoons Cumin seeds
  • 4-6 Byadagi chillies
  • 1 ½ cup grated coconut
  • Tamarind, jaggery and salt as
    mentioned above

    Grind all ingredients for the masala in raw form. In this preparation, it is not required to roast the masala. Add the ground masala to the boiled vegetables and boil for 4-5 minutes in a vessel.

    When compared with the previous preparation of sambar, you can experience a major difference in the flavour. Frying the masala alone, changes the flavour tremendously.

    Varieties of Sambar

    There are different types of sambar that can be prepared with same vegetables. The other variety of sambar that can be prepared is plain sambar or bol-huli (bod huli). We can use the same hard vegetables like lady’s finger, brinjal, mangalore cucumber, white pumpkin, suran (Yam), mixed vegetable, raddish, beetroot etc.

    mentioned above

  • 4-6 Green chilli
  • one small lemon size Tamarind
    or ½ teaspoon paste
  • Salt to taste
  • a pinch of Turmeric powder

    for tempering:

  • 1 ½ teaspoons coconut oil
  • ½ teaspoon mustard
  • ¼ teaspoon urad dal
  • 1-2 red chillies

Add slit green chillies and curry leaves to this and boil for 3 minutes in a vessel. Finally add the tempering.

    The tempering or vaggarane in Kannada, along with asafoetida and coconut oil enhances the flavour of this dish.

    Menaskai or Gojju in Kannada

    This is a very well known dish in Dakshin Kannada community, which is sweetish, sour, and spicy-hot to taste. It is good combination with plain rice as well as with chapatis.

  • 2 Fruits like raw mango
    medium size or one small size
    Pineapple
  • 2 cups Coconut
  • 2 teaspoons Urad dal
  • ½ teaspoon Cumin seeds
  • ¼ teaspoons Coriander seed
  • 12 to 14 Methi seed
  • 2 teaspoons Til or Ellu
    (in kannada)
  • 4-6 Byadagi mirchi
  • 3 teaspoons Jaggery
  • Salt to taste

    for Tempering:

  • 1 teaspoon coconut oil

    with mustard

  • 1-2 red chillies
  • few curry leaves
  • a pinch of Asafoetida (Hing)

    Clean and cut the mangoes into small pieces of one inch size. If you are selecting pineapple, remove the skin and cut into small pieces.

    Roast all masala ingredients together except til, which alone is to be roasted separately.

    To the roasted masala add coconut and grind it to a smooth paste. My grandmother’s advice was that we add roasted til in the end of the grinding or else it looses its aroma. Now keep four cups of water to boil in a vessel and to this add jaggery, salt and turmeric powder. After the water boils, add ground masala and boil for another 4 to 5 minutes. Lower the flame and add mangoes or pineapple pieces and switch-off the stove. While hot, the mangoes or pineapples become soft. Do not boil after adding the fruits. It loses its shape if they are over cooked. Keep this aside for minimum 30 mins. During this time, the mangoes absorb sweet and salt from the liquid and give it sourness to liquid base. Now an excellent combination of sweet, sour, salt and spicy-hot menasakai is ready to serve. Garnish with the tempering and serve with plain rice or chapatis.

    Preparation and cooking time:
    15-20 minutes serve : 6

    Rasam Powder (Saarina Pudi)

    In Kannada, rasam is known as saaru, and the powder is known as saarina pudi. Dakshin Kannada community’s saaru is famous all over South India for its special flavour and aroma.

  • 300 grams Coriander seeds
  • 100 grams Cumin seeds
  • 25 grams Methi seeds
  • 25 grams Urad dal
  • 300 grams Byadgi chilli (spice
    lovers add 50: 50 Guntur chilli
    and byadgi chilli)
  • 5 stems Curry leaves
  • 5 grams Asafoetida (Hing)

    Quantity measure using a cup -

  • 3 cups coriander seeds
  • 1 cup cumin seeds
  • 1/8 cup methi seeds
  • 1/8 cup urad dal

    To get the best flavour, you have to roast each ingredient of the masala, one by one, over a low flame. Each requires different temperature to roast properly. Do not burn the masala. Roast slowly till it reaches a perfect colour and you can smell the pleasant flavour while roasting. Do not put in any oil while roasting. In selecting the chillies, opt for freshness and colour. Chillies are also roasted over a slow flame.

    If you have a microwave oven, roast the chillies in it. You have to roast for 2 minutes but giving a time gap of one minute during the roasting process to get properly fried red chillies. When you are roasting each ingredient on the gas flame, you may add a few drops of refined oil. Grind all the ingredients in a mixer. Put the powder in a clean and dry bottle.

    Always use the proportion of 3:1:1/8:1/8 (coriander seed : cumin seeds : methi urad dal) and chilli equal to coriander seed by weight. Select good byadgi (or Kashmiri) chillies only.

    Some use 2:1 or 4:1 proportions of coriander seeds and cumin seeds. This changes the aroma and taste of the powder. Do not add more methi seeds as the powder will taste bitter.

    Garam Masala Powder

    Garam masala is a famous masala powder used especially in the northern part of our country. It is used every day to make food. But in
    South India, it is only used when we prepare north Indian dishes or some rice items.

    In the market, 30 to 35 varieties of garam masala are available.

  • 4 grams Marathi moggu

  • 3 piece Star anise

    All these ingredients are basic requirements to prepare garam masala. You can modify this by adding or omitting spices according to your taste.

    The masala is used in very little amount and added at the end of your cooking to retain its flavour in the dish. The name garam masala itself suggests “hot & spicy”. But it is more aromatic than spicy.

    Coconut milk Ganji

    This is one of the most favourite dishes in Dakshin Kannada comunity. On Tulsi Dwadashi, 12th day after Deepavali this dish is prepared everywhere in Dakshin Kannada families including in temples as prasadam.

    Preparation of coconut milk

    In this dish the major task is removing milk from fresh coconut. A fresh coconut which is perfectly grown on the tree neither tender nor dry, is the right coconut to remove milk.

    It is easy for those who have their own coconut tree. This type of coconut will yield more milk.

    Scrape white portion of one coconut and grind in liquidiser adding little water. Sieve the coconut paste and extract milk.

    Continue for 2-3 times and extract all the milk. One good big coconut gives 4 glass of thick milk.

    Preparation of Coconut-milk Ganji

    It is very easy if you keep coconut milk ready. Cook soft, normal or basamati rice in a cooker on low fire. Add coconut milk to the cooked rice and boil for another 3-4 minutes in a vessel.

    Add a little salt to it. Now serve with salt and adangai uppinkai. Enjoy the food with the pleasant aroma of coconut and special aromatic pickle.

    Boiled Mango Chutney

    This is one of most famous chutney usually known as kukku bajji in tulu made with boiled mango.The procedure starts from boiling the mangoes and preserving it during mango season.

  • 1 Mango boiled
  • ½ cup Fresh coconut
  • ½ cup Curd
  • ½ cup Milk
  • 3-4 Green chillies

    for tempering or vaggarane:

  • 2 teaspoons coconut oil
  • ½ teaspoon mustard
  • ½ teaspoon urad dal
  • 1 red chilli
  • a pinch of Asafoetida (Hing)

    Procedure of boiling Mango

    Select 8-10 good quality raw mangoes mostly of same size during mango season. Keep in a vessel, 3-4 litres of water to boil. While water starts to boil, go on adding whole mangoes to the water. Continue to boil for 5 minutes, till you notice the outer skin of mango starts changing to yellowish green. Remove the mangoes from water and allow it to cool. In another vessel, mix 300 grams of salt with 2 litres of water and keep it to boil. Boil for 10-15 minutes. A layer of salt is seen above the water. Turn off the flame and allow the water to cool to room temperature. Now, preserve the mangoes in the cooled salt water, in a clean bottle.

    The mangoes must be soaked completely in the salt water. These mangoes are used to make the above chutney or bajji until the coming mango season.

    If the mangoes are soft, then mash it by hand. Otherwise, mash it in mixer by cutting it into small pieces. Grind fresh grated coconut to a smooth paste. Add it to the mashed mangoes. Add curds and milk to the prepared mango paste. If the mangoes are sour, then add more milk to it. If they are excessively salty, then add more curds to it. Do not add extra salt because this must have already absorbed salt from water. Add chopped green chillies and temper with asafoetida for aroma.

    Your dish is ready to serve with hot rice. Masala mirchi (curd chilli) will add more flavour to your lunch.

    Do not forget to prepare more rice because the consumption will be double, with this tasty dish.

  • 1 ½ cups (400 ml) Curd

  • 1 inch ginger both chopped
  • 1 cup Coriander leaves
    cleaned and chopped
  • Salt to taste
  • ¼ teaspoon baking soda

    for tempering:

  • 2 teaspoons oil
  • 1 teaspoon mustard
  • 1 teaspoon urad dal
  • curry leaves
  • ¼ teaspoon Turmeric powder

    Heat a little oil and temper it with mustard, urad dal and wait till it splutters. Add green chilli, and chopped ginger. Add rava and roast for a couple of minute.

    Cool it to normal temperature. Add salt and baking soda and mix thoroughly. Beat the curd adding ½ cup of water and add it to the ready rava mixture. Stir well adding chopped coriander leaves, grated carrot and fresh grated coconut.

    Place in the greased idli vessel and steam cook it in a cooker for 10 minutes.

    Serve with sagu and chutney or any one.

    • Always before steaming the idlis keep the cooking vessel on the gas with atleast glasses of water boiling in it.
    • First 6-8 min. cook it on a high flame. Sim it and keep another 10 min. for rice idli and 5 min. for rava idli.
    • For proper shape allow the idli to cool for a minute.
    • Don’t allow the batter to sour further. Refrigerate it after use.
    • You can reduce the sourness by adding 1 teaspoon of sugar.

    Preservation of Rava

    It is always better to roast the rava after bringing it from the market. It has two benefits.

    One, it increases the shelf life and another, it reduces preparation time. You can heat it in the microwave also. Once in two minutes you remove from the oven stir it upside down and keep for another two minutes. Within 5-6 minutes your rava is ready for use. Other method is on a low flame heat it till it changes its colour by stirring it constantly so that the moisture goes out of it. Cool it and store it. This rava will stay for 40 to 45 days without spoiling in normal weather.

    With this rava you can make rava idli, upma, kesari bath or shira and you can save minimum 5-6 minutes in every preparation.

    Chakkuli (Karnataka) or muruku (Tamil Nadu) is an equally famous dry snack. There are lot of varieties in preparing this dish. However, the major ingredients used to make chakkuli are urad dal and rice.

    Laddoos

    Making of Jaggery Syrup

    In any laddoo two important things are there. One is preparation of the main ingredients which are used to make laddoo. The other thing is preparation of the syrup from jaggery or sugar to bind the ingredients. Mostly the difficult part in this is preparation of the syrup. If it is spoiled then the whole laddoo will spoil. Once you learn the trick of making the syrup then it is very simple to do the laddoo. If there is any difficulty then try couple of times, you will get the perfect method.

    Powder ½ kg of jaggery, mix with half cup of water. Heat the mixture on a low flame. With a spoon stir it continuously till it dissolves. Now the mixture starts boiling. Stir it continuously. Keep ½ cup of cold water in a cup. Pour boiling syrup drop in the cold water. Observe it. It must stay like a thick pearl in the water. If it dissolves in the water then the syrup is not done. Till it stays just like a pearl in the water, we have to boil the syrup.

    After the syrup is ready put the other ingredients and mix it well immediately.

    If the syrup is perfect then it will take only 5-10 minutes to cool and we can give perfect shape to the laddoo.

    Sugar Syrup

    We have to follow the same way to make the sugar syrup like jaggery syrup.

    Add ½ cup of water to soak the ½ kg of sugar. Heat it to boil on a very low flame. Stir well till all sugar dissolves and starts boiling. Put the syrup in ½ cup of cold water drop by drop intermittently till it stays like pearl. Now the syrup is ready for making laddoos.

    For both the syrup it will take only 5-7 minutes to prepare the syrup.

    40-50 medium size laddu’s are done from every kg
    of total ingredients.

    Badusha (Balushahi)

  • ½ cup Ghee
  • 1 cup Curd
  • a pinch of Salt
  • Oil to fry

Fry till it changes to golden brown colour. Add sugar to half cup of water and make thick syrup and keep it ready before frying. Place the fried badusha in the syrup and allow it for 4-5 minutes to suck the syrup. Remove from the syrup keep to dry. Continue this procedure. If you wish garnish with badam and pista.

    Yugadi or Ugadi or Gudi Padava or New Year

    Yuga means era and adi means beginning. Yugadi means first day of the New Year. In the terminology used by the lunar calendar, Chandramana Yugadi falls
    on Chaitra Shudha Pratipada or Padya. This day ushers in the New Year.

    On Yugadi, one must apply oil to the whole body and have a nice bath and adorn ones self with new clothes, jewellery etc. This is a social gathering where family members welcome the New Year with celebrations. On this day, they eat Bevu Bella (Neem and Jaggery).

    Neem leaves, buds and flowers are bitter and these signify sadness. Jaggery signifies happiness. Both symbolize the fact that life is a mixture of sadness and happiness which should be accepted together with equanimity throughout the coming New Year.

    This is the beginning of Vasanth Rithu (Spring) and this heralds the beginning of new life with new shoots, new leaves and flowers. The season symbolises the
    vibrancy of life in the verdant green meadows.

    The Sun crosses the Equator on this day. This is the astronomical New Year and
    is known as “Souramana Yugadi”. This day is celebrated as New Year in South Canara and Tamil Nadu and as Vishu in Kerala.

    In South Canara and Kerala, they will keep khani in front of God. In a plate, the devotee keeps the khani - rice, couple of coconuts, new fruits and vegetables and panchangam (Hindu calendar) decorated with ornaments, cash and a mirror. Early morning, one must see this khani’s image in the mirror. This signifies the belief of elimination of bad omen during the forthcoming year and brings prosperity, health, and good luck to the beholder. The cash kept in it is distributed among all members of the family by the head of the family.

    In North Karnataka and Maharashtra, they celebrate by hoisting a long bamboo shoot on the right side of the main entrance. The tip of the bamboo is adorned with a bright coloured cloth over which sweet sugar crystals, neem leaves, mango leaves and a garland of red flowers are kept. A silver or copper pot is placed in an inverted position over it. This is known as Gudi and this is believed to ward off evil, inviting prosperity and good luck into the house.

    On this day, the new Panchagam (Hindu calendar) is read out by scholars in holy places and temples.

    Kannadiga’s enjoy Yugadi with Kadale Bele Obbattu (chana dal holige) and raw
    mango chitranna while Maharashtrian’s celebrate with Poori and Srikhand.

On this day, women of the family wear new cloths and ornaments and clean the main door (hostilu) and decorate the floor in front of it with rangoli, flowers, beetal leaf and bananas. Special decoration is done with horse-gram germinated small plants. This pooja is done to welcome Goddess Lakshmi to usher in prosperity, wealth and cheer.

    Tempering

    In Indian cooking, tempering is done either at the beginning of the cooking process or as a final flavouring. Tempering in Hindi is known as ‘tadka’ or ‘chunk’, in Marathi as ‘podani’ and in Kannada as ‘vaggarane’. It depends on the area of origin. In Kerala and South Canara, coconut oil is used as the base. Whereas in Tamil Nadu, gingelly or sesame oil is used and in some parts of North India, mustard oil is used. In other parts of our country, groundnut oil is used for the same purpose. But now most of these oils are replaced by refined sunflower oil.

    In South India, mustard, urad dal and dry red chillies are added. In North India, cumin seeds replaces the urad dal. Asafoetida and curry leaves used in this process not only enhances the flavour of the dish, but also aids in digestion. This heated oil or ghee helps to extract the essence and aromas of the spices and herbs. When added to any dish, the aroma and flavour gets enhanced.

    Method

    For South Indian dishes : Heat 1 to 1½ teaspoons of oil or ghee. To this, add ½ teaspoon of mustard, ½ teaspoon of urad dal and 1 dry red chilli. Wait till mustard splutters. Add a pinch of asafoetida and a few curry leaves in the end. This can be prepared just before adding to any dish.

    For North Indian dishes : Replace urad dal with ½ teaspoon of cumin seeds and avoid using curry leaves
