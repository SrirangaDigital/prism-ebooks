Translator’s note

It is comparatively less difficult to translate from one Indian language into another as there is a common cultural core and code. But to translate from an Indian Language into English presents some special difficulties. The very structure of an uninflected language like modern English presents special problems of word-order and length of sentences. When the subject is culture-specific the task is really arduous especially when the text itself is about culture. DVG’s encyclopaedic knowledge and phenomenal erudition do not make things easy. His range of references indicate the scope of this treatise on culture- Amarakosha, Manudharma Sastra, the Upanishads, the Bhagavadgtia, Apastambha Sutra, the Ramayana, the Mahabharata, Bhartihari, Bhavabhuti – to mention just a few sources. It is a task which requires knowledge of Sanskrit on the one hand and more than a nodding acquaintance with Indian metaphysics on the other. I was utterly humbled when Padmasri Dr. Mathur Krishnamurti, Director of Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan asked me to translate DVG’s Samskriti into English. I know I have not been able to vindicate his trust in my ability but I only hope that I have not completely betrayed that trust. I am greatful to my son Dr. S. Ranganath, Director, R.V. Institute of Sanskrit Studies for reading the proof.

S. Ramaswamy
Fellow. Silliman College
Yale University.

About the translator

S. RAMASWAMY (b. 1932) obtained his M.A. first class inEnglish Literature and received the Gold Medal from Mysore University in 1954. He obtained his Ph.D. from Bangalore University (1973). He was three times a Fulbrighter, at the University of California, Los Angeles (1964-66), again, a Senior Fulbright Fellow in California (1988) and a third Fulbright at the Universities of Yale and Texas (1991), when he was made a Fellow of Silliman College, Yale University.

He earned his Masters Degree in American Literature from the University of California (1966) and won the prestigeous Phi Beta Kappa InternationalAward in 1965. He was a Shastri Indo-Canadian Scholar at McGill University, Montreal (1989). He was twice a British Council Scholar at Oxford (1983) and London (1989). He has seven books and more than two hundred papers in English to his credit. He has been widely published in the areas of American Literature, Comparative Literature and Indological Studies. He has been published in Jounals like the Indian Journal of English Studies, Indian Journal of American Studies, Indian Journal of Canadian Studies (India) and International Journal of Canadian Studies (Ottawa), Journal of Common wealth Literature (Oxford), and Remarkings. He has studied Sanskrit traditionally, and has researched in Harvard and Heidelberg. He is a Life Member of Indian Association for American Studies, Indian Association for Canadian Studies, Indian Society for Commonwealth Studies and Hon. Member of the Shakespeare Society of Eastern India, Kolkata.
