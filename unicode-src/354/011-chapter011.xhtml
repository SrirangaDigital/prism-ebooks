Pranayama

Prana is not just breathing. Breathing is the external sign of Prana. Yogis can live without breathing for a long time. When the Prana leaves the body, breathing stops and that body is then called dead. Since we have Prana in the body we are called “Prani.”

Pra means prakrista, which means special. Ana means bala, meaning strength. That which gives strength to us is Prana. There are three Pranas – Mukhya Prana (main Prana), Madhyama Prana (middle level Prana) and Adhama Prana (lower Prana). The Supreme indweller, the Lord is Mukhya Prana. Hanuman, who is the vehicle of the Lord, is Madhyama Prana. The individual soul is called Adhama Prana. The Madhyama Prana (or simply Prana) is the Guru for all souls (jivas) and takes them to the Mukhya Prana. Prana takes the individual soul after death to the appropriate destination according to the soul’s karma. There is no rest for Prana, who makes us breathe even when we are asleep.

Prana protects the soul even at the time of universal dissolution. When the Mukhya Prana (Lord) decides to leave the body, Prana also leaves the body. The Shat Prastnopanishad says that the Lord wanted a perfect personal assistant and he chose Prana. In Vishnu Sahsranama, the Lord has been described twice as using Prana as His vehicle — Vayu Vahana. The Vedas refer to Prana as dhoo, meaning that, death does not come near him. Death is scared to come near those who practise Pranayama.

Our scriptures say that we have various deities who function in the body, like the Sun in the eyes, the Moon in the ears, the Aswini Kumaras in our nostrils, Agni in our mouth, Shiva in the mind, etc. These deities can function in our body only when we have the Prana (vital air) in the body. When the Prana leaves the body, all other deities cannot function any more. Pranayama means getting the grace of Mukhya Prana (the Lord) and Madhyama Prana (Hanuman) so that Adhama Prana’s (the jiva’s or soul’s) journey towards the Lord continues without any hindrances.

In the fourth chapter of the Bhagawat Gita, Pranayama is defined as Apane juhvati Pranam Pranapane tathapare Pranapana gatee rudhwa Pranayamaparayanaha -‘Yogis join the downward flowing Prana with the upward flowing Prana’, regulating the rate of inhalation and exhalation is Pranayama.

Patanjali defines Pranayama as Tasmin Sati Swasa Praswasayorgati Vicheedanah Pranayama — ‘After mastering the posture, one must practise control of Prana by regulating the inhalation and exhalation.’ Patanjali further describes the benefits of Pranayama as Tatah Ksheeyate Prakashaavaranam — The result of Pranayama is that the covering on the inner Light is removed. According to the Gheranda Samhita, the body becomes light and energetic by doing Pranayama correctly.

There are 4 phases in Pranayama. They are Puraka (inhale), Rechaka (exhale), Anthar Kumbhaka (retaining air inside) and Bhahya Kumbhaka (holding air outside).

By doing Pranayama, all 10 Pranas are energized. There are 5 main Pranas and 5 subtle Pranas (Upa Pranas). The Pranas and their functions are given below:

Main Prana Upa Prana(subtle Prana)
Prana: Mukha indriya — the whole face indriaya - eyes, ears, nose, tongue, skin. Dhanajaya: Opening and closing of the heart valve; protection of the body even after death
Apana: Anus — Excretion. Naga: Burping
Vyana: Affects the whole body – Gives movement to joints. Devadatta: Yawning
Udana: Throat – Speech, swallowing. Guides the soul at the time of death. Takes our mind to higher levels. Krikala: Sneezing
Samana: Navel—Digestion and distribution of food Kurma: Blinking

Humans normally breathe 15-20 times a minute. Rabbits breathe 60-70 times a minute. Elephants breathe 5–6 times a minute. Tortoises breathe 2–3 times a minute. Rabbits live for 3–5 years. Elephants live for over 150 years. The tortoise lives for about 250 years. There is a direct correlation between lifespan and the rate of breathing. By reducing the breathing rate, life expectancy increases. Lifespan is measured in terms of the number of breaths one takes and not by the number of years one lives. Some mosquitos have a life span of only 6 hours. In 6 hours they complete their birth, growth, maturity and death.

It is said in Shrimad Bhagawatam that the creator Brahma’s one second is equal to 100,000 years of humans. Brahma’s 100 years is 3,11040,0000,000,00 (31,104 billion) human years. After Brahma’s 100 years of life span, he gets liberated and takes all the liberated souls along with him to Vaikunta (place with no defects) where he will be replaced by another Brahma. Everyone lives for 100 years but on a different scale. We have heard about yogis living for hundreds of years. This is because they knew the art of breathing. The slower we breathe, the longer is our life span. When we are stressed or angry, our breathing rate increases, which in turn reduces our life span.

According to the scriptures, when we are relaxed it takes 2 seconds for inhalation and 2 seconds for exhalation. For one cycle of inhalation and exhalation, it takes 4 seconds. In one minute we breathe 15 times. In one hour we breathe 900 times and in one day we breathe 21,600 times. When we breathe 21,600 times, it is 1 day for the body. Instead of taking 4 seconds per cycle, by Pranayama practise if we take 8 seconds, it takes 2 days on the external clock to complete 21,600 breaths, which is one day by our inner clock. Our inner clock (body) may say 100 years, but the outer clock says 200 years. If we take 16 seconds per cycle, for 100 years of the inner clock, the outer clock says 400 years. Instead, if we are under stress and prone to anger, we take 2 seconds per breath cycle, to do 21,600 breaths it takes only 12 hours on the external clock. It may be 100 years for the body, but the outer clock has done only 50 years. In short, the slower we breathe, the longer we live.

Breathing with awareness is Pranayama. Pranayama slows down our breathing and reveals the Inner Light by removing its covering. In kumbhaka (breath retention), the body function goes into hibernation. By doing Pranayama one is able to live one’s full life span without disease. When we breathe slowly, our mind de-stresses itself and gets relaxed. The relaxed mind expands. An expanded mind is creative. Creativity is one of the hallmarks of a true leader. Pranayama makes us lead our life well. When we lead our life well, we also inspire others.

Hata Yoga

In the human body there are 72,000 nadis (energy carrying nerves) of which 36,000 are called female nadis (Ida) and 36,000 are called as male nadis (Pingala). Wherever the nadis meet, they form an energy centre called a chakra. Generally, energy does not flow properly in the body because of impurities. This is cleaned by the process of various asanas, Pranayams and Yoga Kriyas.

In Hata yoga, Ha means Sun and Ta means Moon. Hata also means +ve and –ve energy. Hata yoga brings Sun and Moon centres together so that Prana moves through the Sushumnanadi (central nadi). Although we have two nostrils, the breathing usually happens only through one nostril. The left nostril is connected to the right brain. It is called Ida nadi (Moon nadi). Breathing in through the left nostril cools down the body. When the body get heated through emotions, Prana moves through the left nostril (Ida) to cool the body down.

The right nostril is connected to the left brain. It is called Pingala nadi (Sun nadi). Breathing in through the right nostril heats up the body. When the body get cooled down through calm and clear thinking, Prana moves through the right nostril (Pingala Nadi) to warm and energize the body.

This alternate switching happens every 2 hours or so. Practising Pranayama makes the Prana travel through the central Sushumna nadi. By bringing the Prana to the Sushumna Nadi (brining sun and moon systems together), our mind will not be disturbed and swing like a pendulum with every passing thought and emotion. This is done through Nasika shuddi (cleaning the nasal passage) and Nadi shuddi (cleaning the nadis) Pranayama.

Hata yoga also means Ida and Pingala. Ha means the Sun system which is Pingala. Ta means the Moon system which is Ida. Pingala is also referred to as the river Yamun a, and Ida is referred to as the river Ganga. Where Ganga and Yamuna meet, that place is called Prayag, and there the third river Saraswati emerges giving it the name Triveni Sangam — the meeting of three rivers. Sushumna nadi is referred to as the river Saraswati. Ida and Pingala run through the spinal column like a DNA helix, and wherever they cross the Sushumna nadi, that place is called an energy centre or chakra. Ida and pingala cross each other along the central Sushumna nadi in seven places which are the seven main chakras — Mooladhara, Swadistana, Manipura, Anahata, Vishudda, Ajna and Sahasrara.

Ida is referred to as feminine and Pingala is referred to as masculine in nature. A person with Ida nadi predominant is more of heart centered person and likely to be a creative person. A person with Pingala nadi predominant is a more head centered person. The Pingala predominant person is very rational in approach and the ida predominant person is very emotional in approach. To discover Self realization we need to have a balanced Ida and Pingala -well developed in head and heart — only then the well developed philosopher in us comes out. We need to be Ardha Naareeswara — half male and half female personality for realization of God. Realization does not happen to one who is only either a head or a heart centered person.

Breathing through alternate nostrils activates the left and right brains. Pranayama activates not only Ida, Pingala and Sushumna nadis but also activates the energy centres (chakras) in the body.

The postures recommended for practice are Siddasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana and Vajrasana. Pictures 159—162 show these postures. Nadi Shodana Pranayama

➲ Meaning

Shodana means purifying. Nadi Shodana Pranayama is the technique of purifying the nadis.

☞Benefits

Purifies the nadis. As nadis are purified, the energy level in the body increases and the breathing deepens. When the nadis are cleansed, we are able to hear the anahata sound (inner sound). This harmonizes the blood pressure which in turn relaxes the mind. The relaxed mind expands and intuition dawns in an expanded mind. This leads to the opening up of Vijnanamaya kosha. In Vijananamaya kosha intuition starts working and we get flashes of inspiration, which leads us to the Anandamaya kosha. When the mind is expanded we are able to tap into our bliss within.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddasana, Padmasana (lotus posture), Ardha Padmasana (half lotus posture) or Vajrasana (Pictures 159—162a)
  • If you cannot sit on the floor you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in one line.
  • Hold the chin up.
  • Use only your thumb and ring finger to close the right and left nostrils.
  • The thumb indicates the fire element and this is used to close the right nostril.
  • The right nostril represents Surya (the Sun) nadi (Pingala nadi).
  • The ring finger represents the earth element and this is used to close the left nostril.
  • The left nostril represents Chandra (the Moon) nadi(Ida nadi).
  • Bend the fore and middle fingers of your right hand into Vayu shoonya mudra.
  • Bending the index and middle fingers to vayu shoonya mudra alleviates body pain and joint pain, strengthens the heart muscles and reduces earache.
  • Bring the left thumb and left index finger together in chin mudra on your left knee.
  • With the right thumb close the right nostril and inhale through the left nostril to the count of 10.
  • Feel the expansion in the abdomen and lungs as you inhale.
  • With the right ring finger close the left nostril and exhale through the right nostril to the count of 15.
  • Feel the contraction in the chest and stomach as you exhale.
  • Inhale through the right nostril to the count of 10 keeping the left nostril closed with the right ring finger.
  • Exhale through the left nostril for the count of 15 keeping the right nostril closed using the thumb.
  • This is one round.
  • Do 10 rounds of alternate nostril breathing and relax.
  • Slowly increase the duration to 15 minutes or longer and feel the serenity in the mind. Please refer to Picture 163.

Bhramari Pranayama: Type 1

➲ Meaning

Bhramari means bumble bee.

☞Benefits

This is a laghu (light) Pranayama. It is beneficial for people with blocked ears and insomnia problem. It activates the thyroid and parathyroid glands, strengthens the heart, removes angina pain, removes ear pain, improves hearing, reduces blood pressure, regulates heartbeat and strengthens the bones. It helps in hearing the Anahata nada (the inner sound) and helps in meditation.

This Pranayama is performed with the sound of the male bumble bee.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana or Vajrasana as shown in pictures 159—162a.
  • If you cannot sit on the floor you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in one line.
  • Hold the chin up.
  • Bring your thumb and middle finger together into akasha mudra as shown in Picture 174.
  • This mudra strengthens the heart muscles and bones, improves hearing, reduces blood pressure and regulates the heartbeat Raise your hands so that the elbows are parallel to the ground and close both ears with your index fingers.
  • Inhale deeply and as you exhale say the three syllable OM aloud “A”, “U,” and “M”.
  • Say ‘A’ (sounds like ahhh) for the count of 5, ‘U’ (sounds like ooo) for the count of 10 and ‘M’ (sounds like mmm) with lips together for the count of 15.
  • As you are saying ‘M’, gently move your index fingers in and out of the ears and focus on the humming sound.
  • Pause for 10 seconds and try to hear the inner sound – anahata sound.
  • Inhale and repeat the same for 5 minutes and relax. Please refer to picture 164.

⚠ Caution

Keep the nails on your index fingers short.

5. Bastrika Pranayama Type 1

➲ Meaning

Basti means bladder region. Bastrika means bellows. The bladder region moves in and out as in the case of a bellow.

☞Benefits

Benefits:This Pranayama strengthens the bladder region, activates the manipura chakra and oxygenates the whole body. It increases the body’s Prana level and lung capacity. It helps people with breathing disorders such as asthma, purifies the nadis, heats up the body and helps to burn fat. It strengthens and balances the nervous system and relaxes the mind.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddhasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana or Vajrasana (Pictures 159—162a).
  • If you cannot sit on the floor you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in a straight line.
  • Hold the chin up.
  • Place your hands on your knees.
  • Lean forward slightly to keep your back straight.
  • Inhale and exhale rapidly.
  • The duration of the inhalation and exhalation should be the same.
  • When you inhale abdomen goes out chest expands and when you exhale chest contracts and stomach goes in.
  • Do not bend your neck or back.
  • Do this for one minute and relax.
  • Repeat 3 times.
  • Do not breathe too hard and make sure your shoulders are steady.

⚠ Caution

Stop doing this Pranayama if you feel tired or giddy. This is not recommended for people with heart problems or high blood pressure. Doing Bastrika too long in one stretch may over strain or damage the internal organs. Seek expert advice before doing Bastrika. First timers, please do this Pranayama under supervision.

6. Bastrika Pranayama Type 2

☞Benefits

This Pranayama is more intense than Bastrika Pranayama Type 1. It strengthens the bladder region, activates the manipura chakra, and oxygenates the whole body. It increases the body’s Prana level and lung capacity. It helps people with breathing disorders such as asthma, purifies the nadis, heats up the body and helps to burn fat. It strengthens and balances the nervous system, opens up the rib cage and relaxes the mind.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana or Vajrasana (Pictures 159—162a).
  • If you cannot sit on the floor you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in a straight line.
  • Hold the chin up.
  • Cup your fingers on your shoulders with the elbows pointing down.
  • Inhale and exhale deeply and rapidly.
  • The duration of the inhalation and exhalation should be the same.
  • When you inhale the abdomen goes out and the chest expands. When you exhale the chest contracts and the stomach is pushed in.
  • Do not bend your neck or back.
  • Perform Bastrika for one minute.
  • Now keeping the fingers cupped on the shoulders, raise your elbows across so that the bent arms are parallel to the ground.
  • Perform Bastrika in this position for one minute.
  • Now keeping the fingers cupped on the shoulders, point your elbows towards the ceiling so that the bent forearms are pressing lightly against the sides of your head.
  • Perform Bastrika in this position for one minute and relax.
  • Repeat 3 times.
  • Do not breathe too hard.

⚠ Caution

Stop doing this Pranayama if you feel tired or giddy. This is not recommended for people with heart problems or high blood pressure. Doing Bastrika too long in one stretch may over strain or damage the internal organs. Seek expert advice before doing Bastrika. First timers, please do under supervision.

7. Kapalabhati

☞Benefits

This Pranayama increases radiance in the face, activates the brain, improves intelligence, strengthens and balances the nervous system, relaxes the mind and cleanses the sinus. It increases the lung capacity which brings relief to people with breathing disorder such as asthma, purifies the nadis, heats the body and helps burn the fat.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana or Vajrasana (Pictures 159—162a).
  • If you cannot sit on the floor, you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in a straight line.
  • Hold your chin up.
  • Place your palms on your knees.
  • Lean slightly forward to keep your spine erect.
  • Now simply push the air out through the nostrils while simultaneously pulling the stomach in.
  • Do not inhale actively.
  • Kapalabhati is active exhalation with passive inhalation.
  • Do Kapalabhati for a minute and relax.
  • Repeat 3 times.
  • Do not breathe too hard and ensure that your shoulders are steady.

⚠ Caution

Stop doing this Pranayama if you feel tired or giddy. This is not recommended for people with heart problems or high blood pressure. Doing Kapalabhati for too long in one stretch may strain or injure the internal organs. Please seek expert advice before doing Kalapabhati. First timers, please do this Pranayama under supervision.

8. Ujjayi Pranayama

➲ Meaning

Ujjayi means victorious and happy.

☞Benefits

This Pranayama relaxes the mind and body and lowers blood pressure. It helps people with insomnia and helps in meditation. It increases the energy level in the body by opening up the lungs and aerating the body.

⚙ Process

  • Sit in Siddhasana, Padmasana, Ardha Padmasana or Vajrasana (Pictures 159—162a).
  • If you cannot sit on the floor you can sit on a chair.
  • Keep your body, head and neck in a straight line.
  • Hold the chin up.
  • Bring the thumb and index fingers together in chin mudra as shown in the picture 170and place your hands on the knees.
  • Quickly inhale through both the nostrils and feel the expansion in the chest.
  • As you exhale slowly, bring the chin close to the collarbone and breathe out as slowly as you can with a snoring sound.
  • Hold the breath out (bahya kumbhaka) as long as you can
  • Inhale quickly again with your chin up.
  • Repeat this for 5 minutes and relax.

⚠ Caution

Stop doing this Pranayama if you feel tired or giddy. People with either low or high blood pressure should do this Pranayama with caution.
